Module Intent

This module focuses on maintaining tools, identifying faults, and replacing consumables safely.

Core idea: Good maintenance prevents injuries, improves quality, and extends tool life.

Assessment Focus (Task 2)

Prevent failure

Maintenance reduces tool breakdown, overheating, and unsafe operation.

Protect quality

Sharp, aligned, and clean tools improve finish and dimensional accuracy.

Replace consumables safely

Correct accessory selection and fitting prevent damage and injury.

Escalate defects

Unsafe tools must be tagged out and reported immediately.

Maintenance Rules That Matter

Heat and wear

Friction creates heat. Cleaning and lubrication reduce wear.

Sharpness

Dull bits/discs force the tool and increase overheating risk.

Structural condition

Cracks, chips, and looseness increase likelihood of failure.

Alignment

Tool rests, guards, and vices must be set correctly for safe control.

Daily Tool Care Routine

1
Clean after use

Remove swarf, dust, and residue from tool and work area.

2
Check cooling and movement

Keep ventilation slots clear and moving parts free.

3
Protect against corrosion

Apply light oil to exposed metal surfaces when required.

4
Reset safe adjustments

Leave guards, rests, and clamps in a safe condition for next use.

Consumable Change Procedure

1
Isolate power

Disconnect power before changing any disc, bit, or belt.

2
Select the correct accessory

Match type, size, and RPM rating to the tool and job.

3
Fit and secure

Check direction arrows, seat correctly, and remove fitting keys/tools.

4
Final safety check

Reposition guards and confirm clearance before restart.

Reporting and Tag-Out

Rule: If a defect can affect safety, stop use, tag out, and report.

Tag immediately

Do not leave unsafe tools in circulation.

Record accurately

Log faults and consumable changes if required by workshop procedure.

No continued use

Never keep operating a tool with known defects or damage.

Knowledge Check: Multiple Choice

1) When changing a grinder disc, which step is mandatory first?

Isolate/disconnect the power.

2) Which condition requires immediate tag-out?

Missing guard on a power tool.

3) Why must replacement discs/blades match tool RPM limits?

To prevent overspeed failure and breakage.

4) A tool is overheating repeatedly during normal use. Best action?

Stop, inspect, and report/tag as required.

5) Which statement about maintenance records is correct?

Records help track condition and replacement timing.

6) Before restarting after consumable replacement, you must:

Confirm guard position and clear all tools/keys.

Knowledge Check: Scenario Responses

Scenario 1: Why is regular maintenance important in a workshop?

It keeps tools safe and accurate, and reduces failures that can injure users or damage work.

Scenario 2: Why does a dull drill bit cause overheating?

It increases friction instead of cutting cleanly, generating heat in the bit, tool, and workpiece.

Scenario 3: What must you check before fitting a new grinder disc?

Check the disc type, diameter, RPM rating, and condition, then confirm guard and flange fit.

Scenario 4: A saw blade has missing teeth but still cuts. What should you do?

Stop using it and report/replace it; damaged blades can grab, wander, or fail under load.

Scenario 5: Why should ventilation slots on a power tool be kept clean?

Blocked airflow causes overheating, reducing motor life and increasing electrical risk.

Scenario 6: Why must consumable changes be recorded?

Records support traceability, planned replacement, and evidence that safety procedures were followed.

Scenario 7: What information should be included when tagging out a faulty tool?

Tool ID, fault description, date/time, and the person reporting so repair action can be tracked.
