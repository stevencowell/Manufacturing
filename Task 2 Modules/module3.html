Module Intent

This module covers how to select, inspect, use, and maintain hand tools safely.

Core idea: The right hand tool and a safe technique reduce damage, errors, and injuries.

Assessment Focus (Task 2)

Select the right hand tool

Match tool type to material, shape, and finish requirements.

Inspect before use

Check handles, edges, heads, and moving joints before starting work.

Apply safe technique

Secure the work, control force, and keep hands clear of strike/cut zones.

Maintain and tag correctly

Perform minor safe adjustments and tag out unsafe tools immediately.

Pre-Use Inspection Snapshot

Handles secure with no cracks or splinters.
Cutting edges sharp and undamaged.
Files fitted with a proper handle.
No bent shafts, loose joints, or mushroomed heads.

Safe Use by Tool Type

Cutting tools

Clamp the work, cut with steady strokes, and keep hands clear of the cutting path.

Striking tools

Strike squarely, watch the workpiece, and keep others clear of the swing zone.

Filing

Apply pressure on the forward stroke only and lift on return.

Marking and measuring

Scribe lightly and confirm measurements before cutting.

Holding tools

Use vices or clamps to stabilise work before cutting or striking.

Tool-Specific Checks

Vernier calipers

Jaws clean, slide smooth, lock screw working, and scale readable.

Files

Handle fitted, teeth clear, no bending.

Screwdrivers

Correct tip size, no twisting or rounding.

Snips / shears

Pivot secure, blades sharp, smooth action.

Clamps / vice

Screw thread smooth, jaw faces undamaged, and secure clamping action.

Safe Use Essentials

1
Secure the work

Clamp or vice the workpiece before cutting or striking.

2
Keep hands clear

Stay behind cutting edges and away from strike points.

3
Use controlled force

Let the tool do the work - avoid excessive pressure.

4
Clean and store

Wipe tools down and store them correctly after use.

Fix Now vs Tag Out

Fix now (if safe)

Tighten loose handles or screws, tension hacksaw blades, and oil hinges for smooth movement.

Tag out immediately

Cracked handles, bent shafts, missing handles, mushroomed heads, or chipped cutting edges.

Knowledge Check: Scenario Responses

Scenario 1: Which hand tool is best for trimming excess metal on an inside curve?

A half-round file.

Scenario 2: Why must a file always have a handle before use?

To prevent your hand slipping onto the tang and causing injury.

Scenario 3: Why do you apply pressure only on the forward stroke when filing?

Because the teeth cut only on the forward stroke; pushing back with pressure can damage the file.

Scenario 4: What is the purpose of using a file card?

To clean metal filings from the file teeth so it cuts effectively and safely.

Scenario 5: Why should files not be stored touching one another?

Because the teeth can rub together and become damaged.
