Module 2: WHS Requirements for Tool Use

Task 2 - Tools and Equipment (MEM18001, MEM18002)

Module Intent

This module explains the WHS requirements that apply before and during hand and power tool use.

Core idea: Hazards must be identified, controlled, and communicated before any tool is used.

Assessment Focus (Task 2)

Select PPE from the hazard

Choose PPE based on tool and task risk, not routine or preference.

Complete pre-start checks

Confirm tool condition, guarding, accessory fit, and workspace controls.

Apply formal controls

Use hierarchy of control and JSA/SWMS requirements through the task.

Escalate unsafe conditions

Stop, isolate, tag out, and report when safety controls fail.

PPE Decision Guide

Choose PPE based on the tool and the hazard, not habit.

Always in the workshop

Safety glasses and enclosed leather shoes or safety boots.

Add for the task

Hearing protection for grinders, drills, and cold saws; dust masks for grinding or metal dust.

Handling materials only

Gloves are for carrying and handling materials, not for rotating tools.

Rotating tools rule

No gloves, tie back hair, and secure loose clothing.

Hazard Scan (Before and During)

Do a quick scan before starting, then keep scanning as you work.

Guards fitted and functional.
Leads and cords intact, no frays.
Bench and floor clear of swarf and clutter.
Lighting adequate and workpiece supported.
People nearby aware of the task.
Materials sound and free of defects.

Pre-Start Routine

1
Inspect the tool

Hand tools: cracks, chips, loose handles. Power tools: casing, switches, guards.

2
Check the accessory

Use the correct blade, bit, or disc and confirm it is undamaged.

3
Confirm power safety

Test-and-tag status, intact cords, and RCD use if required.

4
Secure the work

Clamp or vice the workpiece and keep guards in place.

Hierarchy of Control (Most Effective to Least)

1
Eliminate

Remove the hazard completely.

2
Substitute

Use a safer tool or method.

3
Isolate

Separate people from the hazard with distance or barriers.

4
Engineering

Guards, clamps, vices, or machine controls.

5
Administrative

SOPs, signage, supervision, and training.

6
PPE

Last line of defense when other controls cannot remove the hazard.

WHS Consultation

Before you start

Check in with the supervisor and confirm expectations.

During the task

Raise unclear instructions or new hazards immediately.

Toolbox talks

Use safety briefings to confirm methods and PPE.

Using JSA/SWMS in This Task

1
Read each step

Match hazards and controls to the task sequence.

2
Apply the controls

Follow PPE and safe method requirements before starting.

3
Stop if controls are missing

Pause the job and report the issue.

Faults and Tag-Out

Rule: If the tool is unsafe, stop and tag it out immediately.
1
Stop and isolate

Turn the tool off and make the area safe.

2
Tag out

Label the tool so no one else uses it.

3
Report

Notify the supervisor/teacher and record the fault.

Emergency and Housekeeping Essentials

Emergency response

Stop the tool, make the area safe, notify the supervisor, and follow site procedures. Know exits and assembly points.

Housekeeping

Clean swarf and filings regularly, keep cords out of walkways, and benches clear.

Knowledge Check: Multiple Choice

1) Which control sits at the top of the hierarchy of control?

Elimination.

2) When are gloves acceptable in the workshop?

Only during material handling (not on rotating tools).

3) What should you do if a guard is missing from a power tool?

Stop, tag out, and report the fault.

4) When should hazard identification happen?

Continuously throughout the task.

5) Which is a required pre-start check?

Confirm the tool is test-and-tagged.

6) What should you do in an emergency?

Stop work and follow emergency procedures.

Knowledge Check: Scenario Responses

Scenario 1: Why are gloves NOT worn when operating rotating tools?

Because gloves can catch and pull your hand into the rotating tool.

Scenario 2: You notice the grinder guard is missing. What do you do?

Stop work, tag the machine out if required, and report it immediately.

Scenario 3: Why is PPE the lowest level of protection?

Because it doesn't remove or reduce the hazard - it only protects the worker from the hazard.

Scenario 4: When should you speak to your supervisor during a job?

Whenever instructions are unclear, a hazard is identified, or something unexpected happens.

Scenario 5: What information in a JSA and SWMS tells you how to stay safe?

The hazard controls listed for each step of the task.

Scenario 6: Why is tag-out important after finding a broken tool?

So no one else accidentally uses it.

Scenario 7: Why must filings be removed regularly?

It prevents slips, cuts, and contamination of the finished product.
