Module Intent

This module builds the quality habits expected in manufacturing: work to specification, check as you go, and report non-conformance early.

Core idea: Quality is built through the whole process, not just checked at the end.

Assessment Focus (Task 1)

Plan to specification

Start with drawings, tolerances, finish requirements, and required process steps.

Check in process

Use checkpoints during the task so defects are found early.

Record and report

Capture measurements, defects, and corrective actions clearly.

Improve next run

Identify what caused variation and how the process can be improved.

Quality Terms in Practice

Term Meaning in workshop context
Quality planning Define standard before work: dimension, tolerance, finish, due time.
Quality assurance (QA) Set up processes that prevent defects before they happen.
Quality control (QC) Measure and verify outputs against specification.
Conformance Result matches approved specification and acceptance criteria.
Non-conformance Result outside requirement; must be contained, reported, and corrected.

Quality Workflow for Practical Tasks

1
Confirm specification

Read drawing notes, tolerances, and process requirements before setup.

2
Prepare tools and checks

Confirm measuring equipment and fixtures are suitable and serviceable.

3
Run in-process verification

Use checkpoints through the task instead of waiting for final inspection.

4
Record evidence

Capture measurements, observations, and any corrective action taken.

5
Escalate non-conformance

Report out-of-spec results early to reduce rework and waste.

Knowledge Check: Multiple Choice

1) Which option correctly defines QA, QC, and quality improvement?

QA sets the process to prevent defects. QC checks outputs against the standard. Quality improvement changes the process to improve outcomes.

2) Why are in-process checks important?

They detect drift or mistakes early, reducing scrap, rework, and delays compared with finding problems only at final inspection.

3) Which is an example of non-conformance?

Examples include incorrect dimensions outside tolerance, poor surface finish, wrong material grade, or missing required documentation.

4) What should be included when reporting a quality issue?

Include the exact issue, location or part ID, measured evidence, possible impact, and immediate actions taken to contain the problem.

5) What are two consequences of poor quality work?

Increased project costs, longer completion time, and wasted materials.

6) What must you compare to decide if your project conforms?

Compare finished work against the assigned project plans/specifications, including dimensions, finish, and required process steps.

7) Which evidence should be included when reporting non-conformance?

Specific defect description, measured variance from spec, affected part/step, and action taken (containment/correction/reporting).

Knowledge Check: Scenario Responses

Scenario 1: During an in-process check, a hole is 0.6 mm out of tolerance. What do you do and what do you record?

Stop and contain the issue, report it, and record the defect, measured variance, affected step/part, and any corrective action taken.

Scenario 2: A teammate wants to skip a measurement step to save time. How do you respond?

Explain that in-process checks prevent defects and rework, and the step must be completed to meet the specification.

Scenario 3: Identify two quality-improvement strategies you would use on your next project.

Examples: add in-process measurement checkpoints, verify tool condition before cutting, improve sequencing/role allocation, and use clearer handover notes.

Scenario 4: How would you explain that your project conforms to specifications?

Compare the finished dimensions and finish to the project plans/specs, confirm required process steps were followed, and note any checks completed.
