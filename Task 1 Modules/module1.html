Module Intent

This module builds the communication and planning habits required before practical work begins. The focus is on understanding instructions, confirming roles, and sequencing work so the practical task is safe, accurate, and on time.

Core idea: Clear instructions, clear roles, and clear timing reduce rework and safety risks.

Assessment Focus (Task 1)

Interpret workplace information

Read job instructions, plans, and SOPs before selecting tools or starting work.

Communicate in a team

Use clear language, ask when unsure, and report hazards or delays early.

Plan practical workflow

Sequence the task using Gantt/production tally information and checkpoints.

Role Clarity Before Practical Work

Student responsibilities

Follow instructions, confirm measurements, use required PPE, and report hazards or faults.

Supervisor/teacher responsibilities

Provide clear instructions, verify controls, and support safe progress through the task.

Team responsibilities

Share updates, use consistent terminology, and confirm sequence before key steps.

Practical Sequence (Gantt / Production Tally)

  1. Contribute to the group plan and complete the JSA plus Gantt/production tally chart.
  2. Confirm required materials from plans and production tally information.
  3. Cut material to size with the nominated tool to specification.
  4. Measure and mark out accurately before drilling or shaping.
  5. Drill and tap required holes according to plan.
  6. Cut required shapes and features as specified.
  7. Mark tap diameters next to corresponding holes.
  8. Deburr and finish all edges and tapped holes.
  9. Complete final identification and inspection requirements.

Knowledge Check: Multiple Choice

1) Why must you clarify task requirements before starting practical work?

To confirm the required outcome, standards, safety controls, and sequence. This prevents errors, unsafe actions, and wasted materials.

2) Which set correctly matches verbal, written, and visual communication?

Verbal: toolbox talk or supervisor briefing. Written: SOP or job list. Visual: signage, drawings, or hand signals.

3) What is one reason a Gantt-style timeline is useful for practical tasks?

It breaks a task into steps with timing, helping students prioritise work, track progress, and identify delays early.

4) Which statement best describes feedback?

Feedback is specific, respectful, and improvement-focused. Criticism is often vague or personal and usually does not help performance.

5) When should you ask a supervisor or teacher for assistance?

When instructions are unclear, a hazard appears, quality is not meeting standard, equipment is faulty, or progress is slipping from the plan.

6) Which is a common communication barrier in a workshop?

Examples: using language that is too technical or too basic, using uncommon or unclear terms, or giving non-concise directions.

7) How would you gather information about procedures for a work task?

Use all available sources: SDS/Safety Data Sheets, JSA/SWMS, project plans/specs, and supervisor instructions.

Knowledge Check: Scenario Responses

Scenario 1: You are unsure about a measurement on the drawing just before cutting. What do you do?

Stop and clarify with the supervisor/teacher. Re-check the drawing and project plan, confirm the measurement, then proceed once the requirement is clear.

Scenario 2: During a group plan, a teammate uses jargon you do not understand. How do you respond?

Ask for clarification using specific questions and confirm the steps back to the group so everyone has the same understanding.

Scenario 3: You receive a verbal instruction that conflicts with a written SOP. What should you do?

Pause the task, raise the conflict with the supervisor/teacher, and follow the approved SOP unless updated instruction is confirmed.
